Daily plan · build basic strength

Eat steady, move a little, get stronger.

A simple day you can repeat using food you already buy and exercises that need no gym. The aim is a little strength and easy, comfortable eating — nothing extreme, nothing extra to spend.

The day, meal by meal

Food

Five small touch-points across the day. Spreading food out means more total nourishment without any single meal feeling heavy.

  • After waking

    Banana, a slice of cake, a glass of milk

    1 banana · 1 slice walnut-banana cake · 1 glass milk. An easy, filling start with some protein from the milk.

    Banana Cake Milk

  • Mid-morning

    A boiled egg or a handful of peanuts

    1–2 eggs, or one small handful of peanuts. A light protein top-up between meals — no cooking needed.

    Egg Peanuts

  • Lunch

    Rice and chicken, plus a small bowl of dal

    Your usual plate of rice · a good piece of chicken · a small bowl of dal. Eat to comfortable, not full. Dal adds cheap, easy protein.

    Rice + chicken Dal

  • Afternoon

    Milk, yogurt, or peanut butter on bread

    Pick one: 1 glass milk · 1 cup yogurt · or peanut butter on a slice of bread. Liquids and spreads add nourishment without feeling heavy.

    Milk Yogurt PB toast

  • Dinner

    Rice and tuna with a vegetable

    Rice (a smaller scoop is fine if appetite is low) · tuna · any vegetable you have. The vegetable covers fibre and the nutrients the rest of the day misses.

    Tuna Vegetable
✓
The one rule that matters Smaller amounts, more often. Always eat to comfortably satisfied — never to the point of discomfort. Appetite grows slowly over the weeks, so there is no need to push or force anything.

Five moves for basic strength

Exercise

Each move trains one major pattern of the body. Together they cover everything you need — no machines, no long routine. Tap a card to mark it done for the day.

Push-ups

Chest · shoulders · arms

Start easy
Hands on a table or wall; drop to your knees if a full one is too hard.
Progress
Full push-ups on the floor, then feet slightly raised.
2–3 sets · about 8–12 reps

Squats

Thighs · hips

Start easy
Bodyweight squats — sit your hips back as if reaching for a chair.
Progress
Lunges, then hold a backpack loaded with books or bottles.
2–3 sets · about 10–15 reps

Rows (pull)

Back · arms

Start easy
Lie under a sturdy table and pull your chest to the edge, or bend over and pull a loaded bag to your ribs.
Progress
Pull-ups, if you have a bar or strong branch.
2–3 sets · about 8–12 reps

Glute bridges

Hips · lower back · hamstrings

Start easy
Lie on your back, feet flat, and lift your hips into a straight line.
Progress
One leg at a time, the other held out straight.
2–3 sets · about 12–15 reps

Plank

Core · whole midsection

Start easy
Hold a straight line on forearms and toes — rest on your knees to begin.
Progress
Longer holds, then side planks.
2–3 holds · as long as form stays clean
△
How to run it Two or three days a week, with a rest day in between — muscle rebuilds during rest and sleep. Warm up with a few minutes of easy movement, keep your form clean, stop a rep or two before you cannot continue, and stop anything that causes sharp or joint pain.

The strength comes from doing this regularly and slowly adding a little — one more rep, or a harder version. The food simply supports the recovery; you do not need anything special or expensive.

This is general guidance, not medical or coaching advice. Ease in gently and let your body set the pace. If eating still feels uncomfortable over time, it is worth mentioning to a regular doctor.
